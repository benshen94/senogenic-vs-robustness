# Reviewers' Comments

## Reviewer #1 (Remarks to the Author)

This is a very dense article with multiple elements applied to a common theme. Here is a summary as I understand it -- recognizing that I'm not familiar with some of the language but very familiar with the underlying concepts.

When it comes to human longevity (and actually the longevity of other sexually reproducing species), two elements have been of statistical and public health relevance -- maximum lifespan (e.g., the tail of the survival distribution or the longest lived among the longest lived), and mean or median survival, which is basically something akin to average duration of life or period life expectancy at birth. In a genetically heterogeneous population, some population subgroups fall into the tail of the distribution, but most hover around the mean.

The authors correctly observe that the mean has been rising rapidly -- but has slowed down in recent decades; and the maximum has not changed much at all. To illustrate, life expectancy at birth rose by 30 years in the 20th century, but life expectancy at age 90 rose by only 2-3 years. Convergence is occurring at threshold ages later in life -- survival curves are becoming more rectangular. Why?

These are the facts of human longevity -- they cannot be disputed. The questions here then include what molecular/genetic/cellular changes with time contribute to this; does the accumulated damage model in aging predict this observation; what is the role of stochastic events in these survival/death distributions; and what happens when various interventions are brought into play (e.g., control of risk factors and diseases vs rate control of aging itself)?

The authors then bring in other unique elements to address the questions at hand, such as the use of intrinsic mortality by removal of the contaminating effect of extrinsic mortality, and the accelerated aging model of Progeria.

I probably didn't quite do the full theme of this paper justice, but I think I got to the heart of most of it.

Specific comments:

Let me start out by acknowledging the breadth of coverage and difficulty in crafting this article. The authors have taken on a difficult task. As it turns out, the question of rate control vs disease/risk factor modification in relation to mean and maximum lifespan -- the heart of this paper, is not new. The moment I saw this in the paper I was immediately reminded of a book by Neugarten and Havighurst from 1977 (based on a National Academy of Sciences meeting in 1975) [Neugarten, B. L., & Havighurst, R. J. (1977). Extending the human life span: social policy and social ethics (Vol. 770123). Committee on Human Development, University of Chicago]. I would not expect the authors of this paper to know about this book and its contents, and you might even have a difficult time accessing it. But a summary has been published and is available, but my guess is that the authors are unaware of this book chapter as well because it's placement in the literature is not easily accessible. I would strongly encourage the authors to read this [https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-90-481-9996-9.pdf], chapter 27.

Basically, this manuscript now under review revisits the concept of rate control vs disease control through various means, but some rather interesting elements are introduced that separate it from ideas from a half century ago. To be clear to the editors, rate control is modifying the biological process of aging itself, and disease control (the language is different in this paper but the concept is the same) is what happens to mean and maximum longevity through risk factor modification and the manipulation of diseases through medicine and other interventions. Further, the authors introduce an accelerated aging model of progeria -- quite a novel concept; and they deploy their previous work involving the removal of extrinsic mortality from the total mortality schedule (also a difficult job but done admirably and correctly in their recent publication). These refinements are well justified, and in fact, there are very few research teams that are capable of carrying off this type of analysis.

1. The author's use of the Saturating-Removal (SR) model, and the hypothesis that maximum lifespan can only be modified by altering the aging process itself, is also not new -- this was actually hypothesized by Nathan Keyfitz in 1977 (using different language involving life table entropy) and used later in 1990 when estimates of the upper limits to human longevity were addressed; and addressed in detail in the Neugarten and Havighurst book. All I'm suggesting here is that proper scholarship requires reference to this earlier work -- these are minor edits only and this suggestion does not detract from the value of this article.

2. p.2. One quick note on Gompertz. Gompertz himself never applied his 1825 formula to older ages in humans -- he first cut his formula off in the 60s and later in the 80s. This is relevant here because the authors imply that it applies to older ages, but the observed reality is a leveling off. That is, late life mortality deceleration may just be a byproduct of selection where the most robust subgroups survive to later ages where observed mortality risk changes (a conclusion you arrived at on p.5).

3. p.3. A compliment. The use of intrinsic mortality by subtracting the contaminating force of extrinsic mortality is a novel and appropriate analytical challenge here that few research teams can accomplish. Doing so not only adjusts the percentage contribution to longevity from genetics and environment, it also plays an important role in discussions of rate control vs disease control. I have never seen this done anywhere else in the scientific literature when applied to mean and maximum lifespan -- making this a valuable contribution to this research. Also, I like your language on damage kinetics vs robustness stochasticity, but this is close to the concepts covered in the Neugarten and Havighurst book from 1977.

4. p.7. Novel use of NHANES linking lifestyle factors to their influence on lifespan gains -- within the context of rate control vs disease/risk factor control. This confirms decades of research using NHANES that the primary effect is on diseases (robustness parameters) and not aging, which explain why decelerating gains in longevity must occur in long-lived populations.

5. p.9. Interpretation of historical gains in longevity reflecting the declining influence of extrinsic mortality and the improving influence of robustness. While I agree that both of these phenomenon are happening, I would like the authors to consider that modern medical technology that extends life is not achieving lifespan extension only through enhanced robustness -- which I think comes from risk factor modification (which is no doubt occurring); rather, these technologies are manufacturing artificial survival time that does not fit cleanly within either rate control or disease control in the way it's interpreted here. For example, a lung or heart transplant yields artificial survival time -- the beneficiaries are not more robust as implied here -- they're living on manufactured time that often leads to less robustness (a negative tradeoff). They may be alive longer, but they're certainly not more robust. I'm just encouraging the authors to consider the likelihood of the presence of another parameter of artificial survival time that is also subject to diminishing returns, and which generates more years of unhealthy life rather than more healthy life through robustness modification. I believe the evidence involving lifespan/healthspan calculations in recent years in long-lived populations supports the idea that artificial survival time is extending the period of unhealthy life faster than lifespan itself. I don't this discussed here at all.

The remaining parts of the manuscript are well justified. While I'm not familiar with some of the language used, the concepts are presented in an understandable way. I think the authors should consider carefully the concept raised in #5 above. Robustness modification implies that we're always more robust as a result, and the evidence involving relative changes in lifespan vs healthspan does not support this. The idea of artificial survival time such as keeping people alive through artificial means near the end of life, is not discussed at all (unless I missed it).

Overall, this is an important contribution to the literature and a very novel way of explaining why the rise in longevity is slowing down at the population level, and why maximum lifespan is not likely to change unless rate control occurs. As noted earlier, this is not a new concept and I would encourage the authors to go back in time to the discussion that took place at a National Academy of Sciences meeting in 1975 where many of the same issues were discussed (in a far less sophisticated way) -- but the conclusions were basically the same.

S. Jay Olshansky

## Reviewer #2 (Remarks to the Author)

Review of "Invariant damage rates constrain the maximal human lifespan" by Ben Shenhar, Shachaf Frenkl, Tomer Levy, and Uri Alon

Summary of the key results

The authors use (p. 2) a "Saturating-Removal (SR) model, a stochastic model of damage accumulation originally developed from senescent cell dynamics in mice", which they assert "captures key features of aging, including the exponential rise in hazard with slowdown at old age, exponential disease incidence, effect of parabiosis, longevity interventions and their combinations, and aging differences between species". This model treats damage accumulation (p,2) "as a balance between [sic] production that rises with age, removal that saturates at high damage and stochastic noise" and that death occurs when damage crosses a critical threshold. The model has these parameters: production rate, removal rate, noise amplitude, and death threshold.

The authors write (p.2):
Here we show that variation in human lifespan is consistent with person-to-person differences in model parameters under a strong constraint: changes of more than a few percent in damage production or removal rates generate unrealistically long lifespans, whereas variation in death thresholds or stochastic noise, which we term robustness parameters, preserves the observed upper limit. This pattern is supported by analyses of NHANES exposure cohorts, centenarian sibling data, records of the longest-lived individuals, and historical cohorts adjusted for extrinsic mortality. In contrast, survival data from Hutchinson-Gilford progeria indicate altered damage production dynamics. Similar constraints emerge in additional models of aging and mortality. Extrapolating recent increases in robustness predicts diminishing life expectancy gains over the coming century. Together, these findings suggest that damage production and removal rates in humans are tightly bounded, and that substantially extending maximal lifespan will require directly modifying these processes rather than enhancing robustness alone.

Analysis of this model
The model is reasonably well-motivated.. The authors write (p. 14) that analysis of two other models of aging (the Makeham-Gompertz model and Fedichev and Gruber's "minimal model of aging") produce results qualitatively similar to those produced by analysis of the SR model. This concordance is important although it does not by itself provide strong evidence that the results are not "model-dependent" because the models share many assumptions.

Data & methodology: validity of approach, quality of data, quality of presentation
The SR model and parameters fitted to it are poorly described and in some instances are not presented. It is first introduced (p. 4) in a figure and then more fully (p. 22) where the parameters are not even defined. In the former instance, results are shown and the parameter values are given ("Control parameters eta = 0.5 year-2, beta = 54.7 year-1, kappa = 0.5, epsilon = 51.8 year-1, Xc = 17" but there is no basis given for these values. On page 6, one sees parameter values "eta = 0.43 year-2, beta = 65.7 year-1, kappa = 0.5, epsilon = 51.83 year-1, Xc = 18" and these are described as being "fit to Swedish female cohorts (1890-1900)." On page 8, one sees parameter values "eta = 0.54 year-2, beta = 54.75 year-1, kappa = 0.5, epsilon = 51.83 year-1, Xc = 21) with 20% heterogeneity in Xc, based on previous analysis of twin studies", along with a citation of Shenahar et al. (2026). On pages 11, 14, and 29, one sees parameter values "eta = 0.49 year-2, beta = 54.8 year-1, kappa = 0.5, epsilon = 51.8 year-1, Xc = 17" and these are described as being based on "Sweden 2019 mortality data". On page 12, one sees "eta = 0.66 year-2 , beta = 62.05 year-1, kappa = 0.5, epsilon = 51.83 year-1, Xc = 14, with 20% person-to-person variation in threshold Xc." On page 35, one sees "eta = 0.62 years-2, beta = 64 years-1, kappa = 0.5, epsilon = 52 years-1, Xc = 18", with no mention of data. This kind of sloppy presentation adds to the work needed by the reader to understand the article.

Appropriate use of statistics and treatment of uncertainties
The authors address uncertainty in their analysis of lifestyle influences on robustness parameters (Figure 3). Otherwise, they do not address uncertainly. This is an important deficiency of the article. It may be that uncertainty is negligible in, say, the analysis of the effect of SR parameter changes on median lifespan and steepness (Figure 1d) but its magnitude and effect go unmentioned.

Conclusions: robustness, validity, reliability
The authors' conclusion (p. 2) that "these findings suggest that damage production and removal rates in humans are tightly bounded, and that substantially extending maximal lifespan will require directly modifying these processes rather than enhancing" is not justified by the data presented here. In particular, there has been a "substantial" increase in maximal lifespan, as shown in Figure 5a, at least if one assumes that maximal lifespan can be tracked by the age of the oldest living person. The validity of this assumption is dubious, in as much as the age of the oldest living person is subject to huge sampling error (as evidenced by the jump in the age of the oldest person caused by Jean Calment (122) in 1997, Marie-Louise Meilleur (118) in 1998, and Sarah Knauss (119) in 1998. Nonetheless, if the overall trend in the data in the figure is taken at face value, the maximal life span has changed from 110 in 1960 to 117 in the late 2010s. This change is smaller than the change in the median lifespan in many countries during that time, but the increase of seven years looks "substantial" to me. Of course, the ambiguity of my judgement just piggybacks on the ambiguity of the authors who do not provide any basis for what they mean by "substantially extending maximal lifespan".

It is troubling that authors ignore the implications of Figure 5a. They rule out (p. 6) "variations in .....production or removal" as being "consistent with late-life survival curves, maximal lifespan constraints, and mortality convergence" even though the survival curves associated with variation in production and removal predict that the maximal lifespan is approximately 120, which is just a few years short of the maximal life span observed in 2019 (as well as being a lifespan attained by Jean Calment in 1995). In fact, the overall trend implied by the data in Figure 2a predicts that the maximal life span will be about 120 in twenty to twenty-five years. So much for the "constraint" on production and removal of damage. In addition, I would guess that the median life span will look much more constrained then. Either way, the point is that the validity of the central basis for the analysis - that the lability of median and of maximal life span differ - is not evidently true given the data shown here.

Clarity and context: lucidity of abstract/summary, appropriateness of abstract, introduction and conclusions

As noted above, the appropriateness of the conclusions is not evident. In addition, several of the figures are little more than cartoons. For example, Figures 2a purportedly displays survival curves for "Swedish female cohorts" for cohorts born in 1880, 1885, 1890, 1895, 1900, 1905, and 1910. However, none of the realized survival curves are discernable (even after magnification). Nor is it clear why multiple cohorts are presented. It's difficult to overstate how bad this kind of presentation is. The authors point to data and implicitly license the reader to assess how the data relate to the model results but, in fact, the reader cannot do this.

The authors have presented a huge number of analyses, so much so that I think most readers will be overwhelmed. The main text has six figures, all with multiple panels and the SI has three figures, one of which has twelve panels. Yet, most of the figures lack detailed explication in the text. The authors owe the readers and themselves something more at least if the goal is to present science that is independently assessable, as opposed to being presented in such a way that the reader is apparently expected to accept the authors' conclusions at face value.

## Reviewer #3 (Remarks to the Author)

This paper uses the Saturating-Removal (SR) model to interpret patterns in human lifespan, with particular emphasis on the contrast between gains in median lifespan and the apparent stability of maximum lifespan. I found parts of the modelling exercise interesting, especially the attempt to connect survival-curve shape with distinct classes of ageing parameters. However, I am not persuaded that the evidence supports the paper's stronger biological conclusions.

The paper is ambitious and addresses an important question: why improvements in median lifespan have not been accompanied by comparable changes in the upper tail of human lifespan. I found the distinction between robustness-like parameters and damage-production/removal parameters useful as a hypothesis-generating framework. The attempt to confront the same model with lifestyle, historical, familial-longevity, supercentenarian, and progeria data is also valuable, even where I do not think the resulting inferences are fully established.

In its current form, the paper is best understood as a speculative, hypothesis-generating modelling exercise rather than as a demonstrated mechanistic explanation of human lifespan limits. The analyses show that the SR model can organise several empirical patterns in an interesting way, but they do not establish that the model parameters are identifiable from population survival data or that variation in those parameters corresponds to real biological variation across individuals, cohorts, or interventions.

The paper proposes that the SR model can be understood as a realistic dynamic model of individual ageing because "the model generates individual-specific damage trajectories and reproduces the Gompertz law, including late-life mortality deceleration". Reproducing population-level mortality patterns does not by itself establish that the underlying dynamic parameters are identifiable or meaningfully interpretable. There is no substantial engagement with the substantial literature (e.g., Yashin et al. 1994 "A duality in aging", Steinsaltz and Evans 2004 "Markov mortality models", Begun and Yashin 2019 "Study of the bivariate survival data using frailty models based on Levy processes") pointing out that different dynamic models may be observationally equivalent. The cited mouse-intervention research - "In mice, differences in survival curves across interventions map onto specific changes in SR parameters, which differentially affect median lifespan and survival curve shape" - is attributed to an earlier paper by some of the same authors, but this description of the results of that paper overstates what the analysis demonstrates. It shows that qualitative features of observed survival curves can be matched by some choices of SR parameters, but that is a weaker claim than showing that those parameters are uniquely determined or biologically real. The SR model has more structure and flexibility than the low-dimensional survival summaries used to evaluate it, so matching qualitative features of survival curves does not establish parameter uniqueness, and cannot be taken as a persuasive argument for the reality of the model dynamics.

A further concern is that the analysis treats variation in the SR model's chosen parameters as though these coordinates were biologically natural. The paper explores local changes in individual parameters, often one at a time, but genetic, environmental, and historical variation need not align with this parametrisation. Consequently, the conclusion that modest heterogeneity in some parameters would produce unrealistic effects may be partly coordinate-dependent. The biological interpretation of the SR parameter axes therefore needs more justification.

The statement that "late-life survival declines super-exponentially" - a core principle underlying the analysis - needs a precise definition. If this refers to the survival function, it should be distinguished from claims about the hazard. If it refers to mortality rates continuing to rise at extreme ages, the statement should be reconciled with the paper's own emphasis on late-life mortality deceleration and with the literature suggesting mortality plateaus among supercentenarians.

The comparison with centenarian-sibling data is suggestive but not decisive, because the empirical groups are not selected by known causal parameters. Siblings of centenarians are a mixture: some may share longevity-promoting variants, others may not, and the centenarians themselves include both genetically advantaged individuals and individuals who survived through favourable stochastic histories. Mortality convergence in such mixed groups could therefore arise from selection and frailty mixing, independently of the specific SR mechanism. These are being compared with simulated data where the relevant parameter-determined "type" is known and uniform. A more persuasive comparison would simulate the same ascertainment process used in the empirical data.

The analysis of lifestyle effects is interesting, but the inferential step from the steepness-longevity plot to conclusions about specific SR parameters is difficult to evaluate statistically. Exposure groups are placed in a reduced two-dimensional space and compared with model-generated directions corresponding to changes in individual SR parameters. However, it is not clear how closeness to one parameter curve rather than another is quantified, nor how uncertainty is propagated through the full chain of inference: estimating survival curves, fitting and removing extrinsic mortality, reducing the results to steepness and median lifespan, comparing them with SR-generated trajectories, and judging correspondence to particular parameter changes. This concern is compounded by the earlier issue that single-parameter SR perturbations are model-coordinate choices, not independently established biological directions.

The analysis is strongest when presented as a demonstration of how different classes of parameter variation would affect survival-curve shape and the extreme tail. In that form, the model provides a useful organising language for thinking about why some interventions may shift median lifespan more than maximum lifespan.

Overall, I think the manuscript contains an interesting modelling framework, but its central claims substantially exceed what the analyses establish. The SR model provides one possible interpretation of several demographic patterns, not a validated mechanistic explanation of human lifespan limits. I would therefore regard the paper as exploratory and hypothesis-generating rather than suitable, in its present form, for a high-level general journal.
